Exhibit 003 — The same event, seen differently

What does spacetime look like
when you watch it collapse?

Same event

Same neutron star and black hole as exhibit 001 — same masses, same Peters-formula decay, same merger. What's different is how the light gets computed. Here every pixel traces an actual photon path through the Schwarzschild metric. In exhibit 001 the lensing was a screen-space approximation; here it's real integrated geometry.

Per pixel

Every pixel runs up to 300 iterations of the geodesic equation — the photon bends toward the black hole based on its angular momentum and how close it gets, to the fifth power. The distorted starfield behind it all is real: NASA's Deep Star Maps 2020, 1.7 billion stars from Hipparcos-2, Tycho-2, and Gaia DR2, projected onto a sphere.

The shadow

The black disk isn't the event horizon — it's bigger. Any photon that gets within b_crit = √27 × M never escapes, and that gets checked with actual math before the ray marching even starts, so the shadow's edge is mathematically exact, not eyeballed. The bright arc around it is the photon ring: light that nearly made a full orbit before escaping toward you, carrying a squished image of the entire sky.

Disappearance

When the neutron star crosses behind the shadow, it vanishes — not because it's hidden, but because there's genuinely no path for light to reach your eye from its surface without crossing the shadow boundary. When it reappears, its light took a slightly longer route around the black hole to get there. At these distances, a real system would also flash a faint Einstein ring right as the star lines up exactly behind the black hole.
