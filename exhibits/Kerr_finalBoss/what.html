The physics,
somewhat explained and extremely simplified,
because this is basically level 99 of astrophysics

So, what are you actually looking at?

A black hole that spins.
And that one detail changes almost everything — the shape of the shadow, the way light gets dragged around, even how close you can orbit before you're done for.
Real black holes basically all spin, so in a way this one's more real than the static black hole.
Let's get into it.

01

Spin is the default, not the exception

Stars spin and when they collapse, they keep every bit of angular momentum it had. This is then crammed into something a few kilometers wide. So it spins. Fast. Sometimes 100's of times a second.

We measure that spin with a number called a/M, running from 0 (no spin - that's the static black hole exhibit) up to 1 (the theoretical maximum, spinning as hard as physics allows).
Real black holes spin really fast: The black hole in M87 is estimated above 0.9.
Drag the slider in the exhibit and you're moving through that whole range yourself.

Roy Kerr worked out the math for this in 1963 — 47 years after Schwarzschild did the non-spinning version.
Turns out "just add spin" was almost half a century of extra work. True "last boss" energy.

Here's the thing that makes a/M the only slider worth having: the no-hair theorem. It says a black hole is described, completely, by just three numbers — mass, spin, and electric charge. Nothing else survives the collapse. Whatever the star used to look like, whatever it was made of, all of that information is gone, and all that's left is those three numbers. Charge is basically always zero for a real astrophysical black hole, mass just sets the scale, so spin is the one dial that actually changes the shape of the thing. That's the whole reason this exhibit only has one slider.

02

Spacetime gets dragged along for the ride

A spinning black hole doesn't just sit there spinning, it'll drag the space around along with it, like a spoon stirring honey. This is called frame dragging, and it has been observed here on Earth.
NASA's Gravity Probe B measured Earth's own tiny version of frame dragging in orbit. Earth drags spacetime too, just by an almost immeasurable amount.

Around a black hole, "almost immeasurable" becomes "whacky". Every photon near the shadow gets swept sideways by this flow, and that's what smears the starlight into a swirl instead of the clean symmetric ring from the static black hole.
Think of a leaf dropped near a whirlpool. It gets caught in the rotation and spirals. Light does the exact same thing here, except the "water" is space itself.

03

Why the shadow looks lopsided

Why is the black hole funny shaped? One side is flattened, pushed in.
That's the spin.

On the side where the black hole's rotation moves toward you, spacetime is flowing with the light, and the shadow's edge gets dragged inward.
On the other side it bulges back out. The faster the spin, the more lopsided it gets — crank the slider to a/M = 1 and watch the shadow squash into a distinct D-shape.

04

The region where nothing can stand still

Outside the horizon, but still deep in the danger zone, sits the ergosphere. In here, frame dragging isn't just strong, it's total: space itself moves faster than light, in the direction of the spin. Nothing — not light, not anything with mass — can hover in place or move against the rotation. You'd get swept along whether you like it or not. The good news, if you can call it that, is escape is still possible from the ergosphere, just not from the horizon. That's what makes it a different boundary, not just a bigger horizon.

In 1969 Roger Penrose worked out something wild: you can actually steal energy out of a spinning black hole. Drop an object into the ergosphere, split it in two, and throw one half further in while flinging the other back out. Do it right and the piece that comes back out carries more energy than the whole object went in with — the difference gets pulled straight from the black hole's spin. Do this enough times and you'd slow the hole down. Which physically allowes a way to "mine" a black hole for energy.

05

The disk, and why one side is brighter

The glowing disk is gas spiraling in. Its inner edge sits at the ISCO, which is the innermost stable circular orbit — the closest anything can circle before it has no choice but to plunge in. Spin shrinks the ISCO, so a fast black hole lets matter orbit much closer than a still one.

That shrinking only happens one way, though. Gas orbiting with the spin (prograde) gets to creep in much closer as a/M climbs, all the way down to just 1×M at maximum spin. Gas orbiting against the spin (retrograde) barely benefits at all, and actually has to stay further out than in the non-spinning case. Frame dragging helps you if you're going with the flow, and fights you if you're not. This exhibit only renders the prograde disk — the one nature actually tends to produce, since infalling gas naturally settles into spinning the same way as the hole it's falling into.

And notice one side of the disk is blazing while the other's dim. That's real: the side rotating toward you is moving at a huge fraction of light speed, which beams and brightens its light (blueshift), while the retreating side dims and reddens. The arc floating above the shadow? That's the far side of the disk, its light bent up and over the top of the black hole to reach your eye.

Random flex, but not really: Interstellar's black hole famously turned this brightness asymmetry off, because the filmmakers thought a lopsided disk would confuse audiences. This one leaves it on. So in one VERY specific way, this is more accurate than the movie. But in all the other ways, Gargantua still stands undefeated.

06

What's real, and what's the last boss of shaders. That I know of.

The physics. This exhibit integrates the full Kerr metric. Every photon carries a conserved energy and angular momentum, and its path is solved with Hamilton's equations in Kerr-Schild coordinates.
The shadow shape, the swirl, the disk asymmetry — all of it falls out of that one system of equations, evaluated hundreds of times per pixel, every frame. It's the same class of math built for Interstellar's renderer. Theirs is just infinitely cooler.

The cost. This is, and I mean this technically, computationally unhinged.
RK4 integration of curved-spacetime geodesics, live, per pixel. It's why this exhibit runs hot and why your phone would rather you didn't. Best experienced on a desktop with a GPU that has something to prove.

The disk's exact look. The gas brightness and color are hand-tuned for drama within the real physical framework, but the asymmetry is physical, the precise color palette is artistic license.

The spin range. Fully real, with one honest cheat: the slider caps at a/M = 0.998, not the "theoretical" 1.0. That's not laziness, it's physics — infalling gas radiates away angular momentum as it approaches the hole, and that process naturally stalls spin-up right around 0.998 (the Thorne limit). Push past that and the horizon would shrink to zero and you'd get a naked singularity: infinite curvature with nothing hiding it. Most physicists suspect nature forbids this outright — the cosmic censorship hypothesis — so the slider stopping just short of 1 is this exhibit quietly refusing to break physics, not a rendering limitation.
