What you're looking at

A black hole that eats the light
of roughly 1.7 billion stars.

√27 × M

The event horizon is the actual point of no return. But the dark circle you're staring at is bigger than that — anything passing within about 2.6x that radius still curves back in eventually, even if it never touches the horizon. So the black you're seeing is less "the hole itself" and more the no-escape zone around it.

1 equation

Every pixel on your screen is a photon, traced backward from your eye through curved space. One equation decides how much each one bends: acceleration scales with the photon's angular momentum squared, divided by distance to the fifth power. The GPU runs that calculation 300 times per pixel, every single frame, just to draw this.

The ring

The bright ring is mostly real physics: photons that skim close to 1.5x the horizon radius get whipped almost all the way around before escaping, dragging a smeared, compressed image of the whole sky with them. Some of the noise you see right at the edge is the shader being messy, not the universe — fix incoming. The ring itself is the same effect that let astronomers photograph M87* and Sagittarius A*, the first actual black hole images ever taken.

G = c = 1

Physicists set the gravitational constant and the speed of light to 1, just to strip the clutter out of the equations. Standard move, not a cheat. The "toy-units" in the readouts map back to real kilometers through the Schwarzschild radius (Rs = 2GM/c²) — a 5 solar mass black hole works out to about 14.8 km.
